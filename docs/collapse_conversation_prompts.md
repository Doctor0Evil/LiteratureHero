# Collapse Prevention Conversation Prompts (G/E/S/P-Indexed)

This document provides conversation starters and structures for discussing societal collapse and prevention in constructive, de-escalating ways, indexed by the **G/E/S/P** framework:

- **G – Geo/Environmental**
- **E – Economic**
- **S – Social**
- **P – Political**

It also describes the **discourse-stress score** \(d_D\) for each dimension, which LiteratureHero can use to evaluate whether conversations are drifting toward paralyzing doom or toward agency and problem-solving.

---

## 1. Design Principles for Collapse Conversations

The prompts here follow several principles:

1. **Start from care and shared values**  
   Anchor the conversation in mutual concern: safety, future generations, dignity, stability.

2. **Ask open questions**  
   Use “what” and “how” questions to invite reflection instead of yes/no reactions.

3. **Name, then narrow**  
   Move from vague “everything is falling apart” to specific concerns mapped to G, E, S, or P.

4. **Balance risk with agency**  
   Every mention of risk should be paired with at least one plausible action or mitigation pathway.

5. **Respect emotional states**  
   Acknowledge fear, anger, or grief before moving toward analysis or solutions.

These principles align with conflict-resolution, climate communication, and community-building practices that emphasize listening, shared goals, and efficacy rather than escalating fear or polarization.

---

## 2. Discourse-Stress Score \(d_D\)

To make conversations measurable, we define a **discourse-stress score** for each dimension \(D \in \{G,E,S,P\}\).

For a given conversation (or transcript) and dimension \(D\):

- Let \(N_D^{\text{doom}}\) be the number of statements expressing helpless doom, inevitability, or totalization about that dimension.
- Let \(N_D^{\text{agency}}\) be the number of statements expressing concrete actions, reforms, or possibilities for improvement about that dimension.

Then:

\[
d_D = \frac{N_D^{\text{doom}}}{N_D^{\text{doom}} + N_D^{\text{agency}}}
\]

Interpretation:

- \(d_D \approx 1\): conversation is almost entirely doom-oriented with no agency.
- \(d_D \approx 0.5\): mixed doom and agency.
- \(d_D \approx 0\): conversation is action-focused and low in fatalism.

For collapse prevention, the goal is not to drive \(d_D\) to zero (which might minimize risk acknowledgment) but to keep it well below 1, ensuring that recognition of risk is accompanied by discussion of possible responses.

## 3. G-Indexed Prompts (Geo/Environmental)

### 3.1 Opening Questions

- “When you think about the place we live—its climate, water, and land—what are you most worried about over the next 20 years?”
- “What recent environmental events (storms, heatwaves, fires, floods) felt like warnings to you, and why?”
- “If you imagine our city or region in 30 years, what changes in the environment seem most likely to you?”

These questions focus attention on concrete environmental stressors rather than abstract apocalypse, making it easier to link to adaptation and mitigation strategies.

### 3.2 Agency-Oriented Follow-Ups

- “What do you think are the most realistic protections we could add where we live—things like shade, flood defenses, cooling centers, or backup power?”
- “Which local or regional decisions about land use, water, or energy feel most important to get right now?”
- “If we had a small budget to make our neighborhood more resilient, where would you start?”

These prompts are designed to convert G-related doom into ideas about infrastructure, planning, and community action, lowering \(d_G\) by introducing agency statements.

---

## 4. E-Indexed Prompts (Economic)

### 4.1 Opening Questions

- “What signs in the economy make you feel like things could break down—jobs, housing, prices, debt, or something else?”
- “Have you ever felt that the system is too fragile or too unfair to last? What experiences led you there?”
- “Which economic shocks worry you more—global crises, local job losses, or sudden inflation?”

These questions invite detailed concerns rather than broad claims that “the economy will collapse any day now,” helping identify specific E stressors (inequality, precarity, volatility).

### 4.2 Agency-Oriented Follow-Ups

- “If you could change one policy or institution to make the economy feel less fragile, what would it be?”
- “What kinds of safety nets or community supports would make you less anxious about economic shocks?”
- “How do you think we could balance innovation and growth with stability and fairness?”

The goal is to move from economic fatalism (“it’s all rigged, nothing can be done”) toward concrete reforms, cooperative models, or personal/community-level preparations, reducing \(d_E\).

---

## 5. S-Indexed Prompts (Social)

### 5.1 Opening Questions

- “When you look at how people talk and act toward each other, what makes you feel like we’re moving toward or away from a breakdown?”
- “Do you feel more or less connected to your neighbors and community than a few years ago? Why?”
- “What signs of polarization or division feel most dangerous to you—online fights, family arguments, protests, or something else?”

These prompts surface perceived S stress: conflict, loneliness, distrust, and identity clashes, without immediately assigning blame.

### 5.2 Agency-Oriented Follow-Ups

- “What kinds of spaces or activities have you seen that actually bring very different people together in a constructive way?”
- “If you could start one project to reduce tension or isolation in your community, what would it look like?”
- “What conversational norms would help us disagree without dehumanizing each other?”

These questions encourage thinking in terms of social infrastructure—clubs, associations, shared projects—and norms, which can lower social stress and \(d_S\) by shifting focus toward repair and connection.

---

## 6. P-Indexed Prompts (Political)

### 6.1 Opening Questions

- “What political changes or behaviors make you worry that our institutions might not hold up in a crisis?”
- “Which rules or norms do you think are absolutely essential for a functioning society—no matter which party is in charge?”
- “Do you feel the political system is capable of handling big shocks, or not? What experiences shape that view?”

These explore perceptions of legitimacy, rule of law, and governance capacity, without immediately pushing people into partisan identities.

### 6.2 Agency-Oriented Follow-Ups

- “What specific reforms or safeguards would make you more confident that institutions could survive a big shock?”
- “How do you think ordinary people, not just leaders, can help protect basic democratic norms or community decision-making?”
- “Are there political issues where you could imagine working with people who disagree with you on other things?”

These questions aim to lower \(d_P\) by moving from “the system is hopelessly broken” to “here are institutional designs, watchdogs, and culture shifts that could help.”

---

## 7. Cross-Dimensional Prompts

Many collapse fears involve interactions across G, E, S, and P. Cross-dimensional prompts help people see these links and identify leverage points.

### 7.1 Linking G and E

- “How do you see environmental changes affecting jobs, prices, or housing where we live?”
- “If we invest in adaptation—like flood defenses or heat-resilient infrastructure—how could that also support local employment or innovation?”

### 7.2 Linking E and S

- “In what ways do you think economic stress is affecting how people treat one another?”
- “Have you seen examples where economic security or opportunity reduced tension and division?”

### 7.3 Linking S and P

- “How does polarization or mistrust between groups affect your trust in institutions?”
- “What could leaders or communities do differently to reduce the ‘if you win, I lose’ feeling in politics?”

### 7.4 Linking All Four

- “Can you think of a story—real or fictional—where environmental, economic, social, and political stress all interact? What could have been done earlier in that story to change the trajectory?”
- “If you had to pick one dimension to work on first (G, E, S, or P) to prevent collapse where you live, which would it be and why?”

These prompts help participants see complex systems rather than single villains or single triggers, which is central to realistic collapse prevention.

---

## 8. Using Fiction and News as Safe Entry Points

Fictional and news-based prompts allow people to talk about collapse indirectly, which can reduce defensiveness.

### 8.1 Fiction-Based Prompts

- “In [fictional world], what early warning signs did people ignore that led to the catastrophe?”
- “Which characters tried to prevent collapse in that story? What worked or failed, and why?”
- “If you could insert one policy or community project into that fictional world 20 years earlier, what would it be?”

These questions encourage mapping fictional events to G/E/S/P, and then back to real-world analogues, making abstract risks concrete but less personal at first.

### 8.2 News-Based Prompts

- “When you read about [recent disaster, conflict, or crisis], which dimension do you think it primarily affects—G, E, S, or P?”
- “What would it look like for the affected community to lower G, E, S, or P stress after that event?”
- “Do you see any similar early signals where we live?”

This enables practice in coding real events into dimensions and brainstorming responses, supporting analytical skill and agency.

## 9. Conversation Flow Patterns and Scripts

### 9.1 Basic Flow

1. **Check-in and consent**  
   - “Is it okay if we talk about some bigger-picture risks and how we feel about them?”
2. **Elicit concerns** (open prompts)  
   - Map them to G/E/S/P internally or explicitly with the other person.
3. **Reflect and validate**  
   - “It makes sense that you’d feel that way, given X.”
4. **Introduce structure**  
   - “It helps me to think about this in terms of environment, economy, society, and politics—can we sort your concerns into those?”
5. **Explore agency**  
   - “For each of those, what feels most within reach to influence, even in a small way?”
6. **Close with concrete next steps**  
   - “What’s one thing you’d like to read, learn, or do after this conversation?”

### 9.2 Group Discussion Pattern

For groups or workshops:

1. Start with small-group sharing of concerns.
2. Have each group classify concerns into G, E, S, P on a whiteboard or digital board.
3. Ask each group to propose one action at each dimension.
4. Aggregate and discuss patterns—where are people most worried, and where do they see paths forward?

LiteratureHero can model this in text-based settings by synthesizing participants’ contributions into G/E/S/P maps and prompting for actions in each category.

## 10. Integration With Automated Analysis

To connect these prompts with automated G/E/S/P scoring and discourse-stress tracking:

1. **Prompt tagging**  
   - Each prompt in this file can be tagged with its main dimension(s) and intended effect (e.g., “G-open”, “E-agency”, “S-cross”, “P-reflection”).

2. **Conversation logging**  
   - For transcripts, annotate each response as doom-oriented or agency-oriented for each dimension, so that \(N_D^{\text{doom}}\) and \(N_D^{\text{agency}}\) can be counted.

3. **Feedback loops**  
   - If \(d_D\) is trending high in a conversation, LiteratureHero can suggest specific agency-focused prompts from the relevant section to rebalance.

4. **Educational dashboards**  
   - Visualize how often conversations touch each dimension and how balanced doom vs agency talk is over time, using this to refine prompt design.

By combining well-crafted prompts with quantitative tracking of discourse stress and dimension coverage, LiteratureHero can support conversations that acknowledge real risks while building the habits of thought and collaboration that make collapse less likely and recovery more plausible.
